Abstract

Spacecraft propulsion systems are continuously under development to improve efficiency of fuel and to bring new possibilities of deep space exploration. This paper discusses the differences between current propulsion systems such as chemical and ion and how they have been developed to improve rocket mission efficiency. It also goes through new propulsion ideas that are still in early stages of development and are being researched in order to bring new possibilities to deep space travel therefore showing the future of spacecraft propulsion systems.

Introduction

Over time spacecraft propulsion systems have been looking towards greater fuel efficiency, lighter rockets and deeper space travel. A good example is the cryogenic engine which directly improves the efficiency of chemical propulsion by 30% by keeping the fuel at low temperatures (Vaidya, 2023). An intriguing idea that is quite recent is the breakthrough Starshot project which would use light to propel micro or even nano scale spacecraft to the nearest star Proxima b in the Alpha Centuri system which is an exciting concept. Firstly, chemical and ion propulsion is discussed along with a comparison of their efficiency and practical use in rockets and then other means of propulsion are considered such as nuclear thermal propulsion, the cryogenic engine and laser propulsion with the possibilities they bring.

Analysis and discussion

Chemical and ion propulsion

Chemical propulsion uses chemical reactions to propel a rocket. This reaction happens between a fuel and an oxidizer which produces heat that causes a propellant to expand and according to newtons third law the force of the propellant being pushed out will be equal to the thrust the rocket receives to accelerate it (Vaidya, 2023). An advantage of this method of propulsion is that it is powerful and can overcome earth’s gravity, but it has quite a low fuel efficiency of about 30% (Vaidya, 2023). Secondly due to the nature of space travel being over large distances huge amounts of combustible fuel are needed which is not only expensive (due to its relatively low fuel efficiency) but requires the primary and secondary phase vehicles to be large (Vaidya, 2023). This is why research is being done into other means of propulsion such as ion propulsion. In simple terms this works by having a neutral gas bombarded with electrons to create charged ions (Vaidya, 2023). The charged particles accelerate towards the ‘grid’ which has a potential difference and due to the current a force is exerted on them consequently providing the thrust for propulsion (Vaidya, 2023). This method has a 90% fuel efficiency which is much better than chemical propulsion however the force produced is relatively low and is not enough yet to overcome earth’s gravity (Vaidya, 2023).

Nuclear Thermal propulsion

Another type of propulsion development is nuclear thermal propulsion. This provides twice the propellant efficiency of chemical rockets (NASA 2025). This means that less fuel needs to be taken therefore decreasing size and mass of payload meaning more essential supplies can be brought along. It works by using nuclear fission and the heat generated by it is transferred to the propellant which then expands to be propelled out of the nozzle of the spacecraft giving it lift (NASA 2025). Research into nuclear thermal propulsion includes the rover/NERVA program that began in the mid-fifties and ended in 1973 (Buden 1988). During this time 22 reactors were built and tested with different materials. For example, It was found that adding zirconium hydride as a moderator into a highly enriched uranium core meant that the total mass of the system could be decreased as seen in the Pewee reactor (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine 2021). This can be seen in some of the sampling data from reactor and engine tests as shown below. It shows how the Pewee reactor maintained the highest propellant temperature of 2550 (kelvin) for the longest amount of time (875 seconds) which demonstrates a higher burning rate of the fuel meaning an increased performance of the engine. The Cryogenic Engine

A key development of spacecraft propulsion efficiency is the introduction of the cryogenic engine. This engine works by storing fluids at low temperatures, so they are in liquid form, but are gaseous at room temperature (Kitsche 2011). This type of engine looks to be a promising for single stage to orbit flight according to a study and works with a combination of an air breathing mode and a cryogenic mode (Mavapoyil and Kuzhiveli 2025). The air breathing mode takes place at the beginning of flight where oxygen is used for combustion and then switches to cryogenic mode once a predetermined velocity and altitude has been reached (Mavapoyil and Kuzhiveli 2025). As the engine uses multiple types of propulsion it can achieve a higher cost to weight ratio and also reduces space debris which is an environmental benefit (Mavapoyil and Kuzhiveli 2025). Compared to regular chemical propulsion this looks like a much more sustainable and efficient means of propulsion however it is still only in the development stage.

Laser Propulsion: The Breakthrough Starshot Project

Deep space exploration sees many new possibilities due to these advancements because of increased fuel efficiency which results in less weight in fuel carried. Potential to visit neighbouring solar systems has also become investigated due to the breakthrough Starshot project. This project aims to send gram scale spacecraft to neighbouring solar systems by laser propulsion accelerating nano spacecraft to 20% of the speed of light (Peretz and more 2022). According to Abbas research is still ongoing to find an efficient propellant for space flight missions showing that this is still a very new development and that deep space exploration is still very limited for now (Abbas and more 2025).

Conclusion

In conclusion the evolution of spacecraft propulsion systems seems to be heading in the direction of leaving chemical propulsion behind and moving to systems that have a more efficient usage of fuel such as ion propulsion. The main parts of the developments are to decrease fuel load to give space for other mission essentials. Finally, evolution of systems still has not brought us in to deep space exploration yet due to the ideas for it being in early development leaving things like travel to neighbouring stars only achievable in the future.
